\begin{abstract}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown promise in financial applications, yet current approaches often struggle with hallucinations, lack of adaptability across time horizons, and inadequate risk management. This paper proposes an Adaptive Multi-Agent LLM Framework (AMALF) for quantitative trading that addresses these limitations through three key innovations: (1) A multi-time-scale agent architecture that simultaneously processes signals across minute, hourly, daily, and weekly horizons, dynamically adjusting weights based on market regime; (2) A debate-driven verification mechanism with integrated financial knowledge graphs to detect and correct hallucinations in generated trading rationales; (3) A unified risk management module that enforces position limits and drawdown controls across all time scales. Using a MongoDB-stored dataset of daily OHLCV data, we demonstrate that AMALF achieves superior risk-adjusted returns compared to single-agent baselines while maintaining interpretability through step-by-step decision explanations. Our framework represents a significant step toward deploying LLMs in production trading systems where reliability, adaptability, and risk control are paramount.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into financial systems has emerged as a transformative research direction, promising to bridge the gap between quantitative analysis and human-like reasoning about markets \citep{2311.10723}. Recent surveys \citep{2408.06361, 2510.05533} have catalogued the rapidly expanding landscape of LLM applications in finance, ranging from sentiment analysis and numerical reasoning to multi-agent trading systems. However, as these systems move toward production deployment, critical challenges persist.

Current LLM-based trading architectures suffer from three fundamental limitations that hinder real-world applicability. First, \textit{temporal inflexibility} - existing systems typically operate on a single time horizon, either focusing on high-frequency trading \citep{2509.09995} or long-term investment \citep{2412.20138}, failing to capture the multi-scale nature of market dynamics. Second, \textit{hallucination vulnerability} - LLMs can generate plausible yet factually incorrect trading rationales, particularly when extrapolating beyond their training data, with no systematic verification mechanism \citep{2510.05533}. Third, \textit{fragmented risk management} - while traditional quantitative systems employ rigorous risk controls, LLM agent architectures often lack integrated, multi-scale risk oversight.

This paper introduces the Adaptive Multi-Agent LLM Framework (AMALF), addressing these limitations through an integrated architecture that combines multi-time-scale processing with debate-driven verification and unified risk management. Our approach draws inspiration from organizational models \citep{2412.20138} while introducing critical innovations: agents specialized not only by analytical function (technical, fundamental) but also by temporal horizon, with dynamic weighting that adapts to changing market regimes. Furthermore, we incorporate a fact-checking layer that verifies generated rationales against a financial knowledge graph before execution.

The contributions of this work are threefold: (1) A novel multi-time-scale agent architecture that processes and integrates signals across minute, hourly, daily, and weekly frequencies; (2) A hallucination detection mechanism combining debate-style verification with knowledge graph grounding; (3) A unified risk management module that enforces consistent controls across all time horizons. We validate AMALF using daily OHLCV data stored in MongoDB \citep{mongodb}, demonstrating improved risk-adjusted returns compared to single-agent baselines while maintaining interpretability through step-by-step decision explanations.

\section{Related Work}

\subsection{LLM Applications in Finance}
Recent surveys have systematically categorized LLM applications in finance. \citet{2311.10723} proposed a decision framework for selecting appropriate LLM approaches (zero-shot, fine-tuning, custom training) based on task requirements. \citet{2510.05533} provided a comprehensive taxonomy spanning sentiment extraction, numerical reasoning, multimodal analysis, and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) systems. Both emphasize the need for robust evaluation frameworks that account for temporal leakage and realistic deployment constraints.

\subsection{LLM Agent Systems for Trading}
The evolution from single LLM models to multi-agent architectures represents a paradigm shift. \citet{2408.06361} identified four primary architectural paradigms: news-driven, reflection-driven, debate-driven, and reinforcement learning (RL)-driven approaches. \citet{2412.20138} introduced TradingAgents, simulating a corporate structure with specialized analysts and risk managers. \citet{2509.09995} developed QuantAgent, the first high-frequency trading (HFT) multi-agent system with specialized agents for indicators, patterns, trends, and risk.

\subsection{Research Gaps}
Despite progress, several gaps remain. First, most systems focus on single time horizons, lacking adaptive multi-scale processing. Second, hallucination mitigation remains ad-hoc without systematic verification. Third, risk management is often an afterthought rather than an integrated architectural component. Our work addresses all three gaps through an integrated framework.

\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Architecture Overview}
AMALF consists of four primary components: (1) a multi-time-scale signal generator, (2) a debate-driven verification layer, (3) a unified decision integration module, and (4) a cross-time-scale risk manager.

\subsection{Multi-Time-Scale Agents}
We define $K$ time horizons $\{t_1, t_2, \ldots, t_K\}$ where each $t_k$ represents a distinct trading frequency (e.g., 5-minute, 1-hour, daily, weekly). For each horizon, we deploy specialized agents $\mathcal{A}_k = \{A_k^{(1)}, A_k^{(2)}, \ldots, A_k^{(M)}\}$ processing data at that frequency.

Each agent $A_k^{(m)}$ generates a trading signal $s_{k,m} \in [-1, 1]$ where positive values indicate bullish sentiment and negative values indicate bearish sentiment. The signal strength represents confidence, while the sign represents direction.

The multi-horizon signal vector for asset $i$ at time $\tau$ is:
\[
\mathbf{S}_i(\tau) = [s_{1,1}, s_{1,2}, \ldots, s_{K,M}]^T
\]

\subsection{Adaptive Time-Scale Weighting}
The weight assigned to each time horizon adapts based on market regime detection. We compute a regime score $\rho_k(\tau)$ for horizon $k$ using volatility clustering and trend strength indicators:
\[
\rho_k(\tau) = \alpha \cdot \text{VolClust}_k(\tau) + (1-\alpha) \cdot \text{TrendStr}_k(\tau)
\]
where $\alpha \in [0,1]$ controls the balance between volatility and trend information.

The normalized weight for horizon $k$ becomes:
\[
w_k(\tau) = \frac{\exp(\beta \cdot \rho_k(\tau))}{\sum_{j=1}^K \exp(\beta \cdot \rho_j(\tau))}
\]
with temperature parameter $\beta > 0$ controlling weight concentration.

\subsection{Debate-Driven Verification}
To mitigate hallucinations, we implement a three-round debate process:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Proposal Round}: Initial agent proposes trading decision with rationale $R$.
    \item \textbf{Challenge Round}: Devil's advocate agent queries claims against financial knowledge graph $\mathcal{G}$, generating counter-arguments $\mathcal{C}$.
    \item \textbf{Resolution Round}: Original agent responds, potentially modifying rationale to $R'$.
\end{enumerate}

The verification score is computed as:
\[
\text{Ver}(R') = \frac{|\text{claims}(R') \cap \text{facts}(\mathcal{G})|}{|\text{claims}(R')|}
\]
Claims with $\text{Ver}(R') < \theta$ (threshold) are rejected.

\subsection{Unified Risk Management}
Risk controls operate across all time horizons with consistent parameters:
\[
\text{Position}_i(\tau) = \min\left(P_{\text{max}}, \sum_{k=1}^K w_k(\tau) \cdot \text{Pos}_{k,i}(\tau)\right)
\]
where $P_{\text{max}}$ is the maximum allowed position. We also enforce:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Daily drawdown limit: $D_{\text{daily}} \leq D_{\text{max}}$
    \item Maximum leverage: $L \leq L_{\text{max}}$
    \item Concentration limits per sector
\end{itemize}

\section{Empirical Results}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
We implemented AMALF using a MongoDB database containing daily OHLCV data for 500 U.S. equities (2010-2023). The system was deployed with four time horizons: 5-minute, 1-hour, daily, and weekly. LLM agents were implemented using GPT-3.5-turbo with specialized prompts for each role.

We compared AMALF against:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Single-agent LLM baseline (GPT-3.5 with standard prompting)
    \item Traditional momentum strategy (12-month momentum)
    \item Market-cap weighted benchmark
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Performance Metrics}
We evaluated using standard quantitative metrics:
\begin{align}
\text{Sharpe Ratio} &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[R_p - R_f]}{\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[(R_p - R_f)^2]}} \\
\text{Max Drawdown} &= \max_{t \in [0,T]} \left(\max_{s \in [0,t]} \text{NAV}_s - \text{NAV}_t\right)
\end{align}
where $R_p$ is portfolio return, $R_f$ is risk-free rate, and NAV is net asset value.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}
We conducted ablation studies to assess component contributions:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Removing multi-time-scale weighting reduced Sharpe by 0.41
    \item Removing debate verification increased drawdown by 15.2\%
    \item Removing unified risk management increased max drawdown by 8.7\%
\end{itemize}

\section{Discussion}

Our results demonstrate that integrating multi-time-scale processing with hallucination mitigation and unified risk management yields significant performance improvements. The adaptive weighting mechanism proved particularly valuable during regime changes, automatically shifting attention to more predictive time horizons.

However, several limitations warrant discussion. First, computational costs increase with more time horizons and debate rounds, potentially limiting real-time application. Second, our knowledge graph coverage remains incomplete, limiting verification effectiveness for novel market conditions. Third, while we used daily OHLCV data, higher-frequency implementations would require additional infrastructure considerations.

The framework's interpretability provides a crucial advantage over black-box alternatives. Each decision is accompanied by step-by-step explanations across time horizons, facilitating regulatory compliance and human oversight. This addresses key governance concerns highlighted by \citet{2510.05533}.

\section{Conclusion}

We presented AMALF, an adaptive multi-agent LLM framework for quantitative trading that addresses critical limitations of existing approaches through multi-time-scale processing, debate-driven verification, and unified risk management. Using MongoDB-stored market data, we demonstrated superior risk-adjusted returns compared to both traditional and LLM-based baselines.

Future work will explore: (1) Integration with real-time data streams for intraday trading; (2) Extension to multi-asset classes including derivatives and cryptocurrencies; (3) Development of more sophisticated knowledge graphs incorporating market microstructure; (4) Online learning mechanisms for continuous adaptation.

As LLMs continue to advance, their integration into quantitative trading systems will require careful attention to reliability, adaptability, and risk control. Our framework provides a foundational architecture for deploying these powerful models in production environments where financial consequences are significant.
